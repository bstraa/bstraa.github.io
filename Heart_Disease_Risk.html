Heart Disease Risk

Posted on Thu 03 March 2016 in articles

Background

Who doesn't like heart disease? Aah-em --- let me rephrase that! Who likes not having heart disease? I recently did a project with a group at Metis where we created a product which would help individuals assess their risk. We had some questions:

  • How can heart disease be predicted?

  • How much do lifestlye choices matter?

  • Is someone with a family history of heart disease doomed?

It is really frustrating not knowing typical numbers when doctors take measurements. What do those numbers mean? Our app makes your diagnostics interpretable! Given user input from our web app (created using Flask), users would receive an overall heart disease risk score from 0 to 100, visualized using D3.js. Below is a snippet of our custom gauge inspired by this.

Acquiring Data & Cleaning

Data was used from the UCI Machine Learning Repository. There's been a lot of academic papers relating to this dataset, so it was accessible by downloading the .csv files and using python's pandas library for cleaning and exploration.

Most of the data had been processed and was (unrealistically!) clean. There were only a few values we needed to impute, so it wasn't long before we could get to exploring.

Initial Exploration

Here's the breakdown of the nominal dependent variable which we are trying to predict. Nominal values can be tricky - the difference between None (far left) and Low (2nd to left) may be much different than from Low to Medium (center).

At this point, we needed to make a decision about how to handle our dependent variable. Nominal variables are tricky - they can be treated as continuous or aggregated as binary outcomes.

We decided to treat our dependent variable as binary - mainly because we wanted to maintain high interpretability for the user. Furthermore, many of the academic papers we were interested in applied the same treatement --- this made comparisons possible.
